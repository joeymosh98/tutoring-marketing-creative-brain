# Australia Paid Social — Winner Insights

This skill is a **pattern library built from 12 proven winning ads** for Tutero Tutoring in the Australian market. It does two things:

1. **Catalogues each winner** — what it is, why it worked, the mechanism behind it.
2. **Codifies a thinking system** — the seven recurring "moves" the winners share, plus a pre-flight checklist for any new ad.

Use this skill to make decisions, not to copy templates. If a new ad doesn't execute at least 3 of the 7 moves, it is unlikely to win.

---

## Part 1 — The 12 Winners, Decoded

Each entry follows the same structure: **Format · Hook · Mechanism · Why it won · Reusable lesson.**

### Winner 1 — "Personalised tutoring for EVERY budget" (UGC video, couple at home)

- **Format:** Vertical UGC video, two people at a kitchen counter, talking-head style with bold caption header.
- **Hook:** "Personalised tutoring for EVERY budget" — leads with the #1 parent objection (cost) and resolves it in the same line.
- **Mechanism:** Objection-handling stacked as ticked benefits — *Transparent pricing · No hidden fees · No lock-in contract*. Each tick is a parent fear neutralised.
- **Why it won:** It doesn't sell tutoring — it sells *low-risk entry*. The CTA "Click here to learn more about Tutero" is soft, not pushy.
- **Reusable lesson:** Lead with the biggest objection, not the biggest benefit. Resolve fear before promising outcome.

### Winner 2 — "Online Tutoring for Prep–Year 12" (8-tutor grid)

- **Format:** Static, 8 tutor headshots in a 4×2 grid, each with first name + one-line personality descriptor ("a passionate educator who amplifies science for all", "an interactive teacher who shows math's real-world relevance").
- **Hook:** Headline does the year-range work ("Prep–Year 12"), the grid does the choice work.
- **Mechanism:** **Choice as proof.** Showing 8 distinct, named, smiling humans signals (a) we have real tutors, (b) we have variety, (c) you'll find one for your kid.
- **Why it won:** Parents subconsciously imagine matching their child to a face. The descriptors do soft personality-matching before the click.
- **Reusable lesson:** When in doubt, show humans — many of them, named, with personality. Faces beat features.

### Winner 3 — "Tutoring has increased her confidence and she spends less time on her phone" (testimonial portrait)

- **Format:** Static, mum smiling, large pull-quote in serif/sans block beside her photo. Tutero logo bottom-right.
- **Hook:** The quote itself — and notice what it sells: **confidence + less screen time**, NOT grades.
- **Mechanism:** **Outcome substitution.** The ad reframes tutoring from "academic improvement" to "my daughter is happier and on her phone less" — two things every Australian parent in 2026 desperately wants.
- **Why it won:** Real face, real quote, and the outcome named is the *non-obvious* one that resonates harder than "better marks".
- **Reusable lesson:** The best testimonials don't praise the product — they describe a *changed home life*. Hunt for these in real reviews.

### Winner 4 — "Online Tutoring for Prep–Year 12" (boy at laptop, ticked benefits)

- **Format:** Static, candid photo of a smiling primary-aged boy at a laptop, three ticked benefits overlaid: *Qualified Teachers & Tutors · Pay Per Lesson · Personalised 1:1 Lessons*.
- **Hook:** Visual = a happy kid actually using it. Headline = the year range (clarifies eligibility instantly).
- **Mechanism:** **Show the outcome as a state, not a promise.** The kid is already happy. The benefits reinforce the three biggest decision drivers: credentials, cost flexibility, personalisation.
- **Why it won:** "Pay Per Lesson" is the killer tick — it removes the lock-in fear that defines this category. Credentials + flexibility + 1:1 is the AU parent decision triad.
- **Reusable lesson:** Three ticks beat five. The middle tick should always be the risk-reducer ("pay per lesson", "no contract", "cancel anytime").

### Winner 5 — "...and it is one-on-one the whole time" (UGC video, two girls on laptop)

- **Format:** Vertical video, two young women on a laptop screen (the lesson itself), captioned testimonial.
- **Hook:** Mid-sentence caption "and it is one-on-one the whole time" — feels like you're eavesdropping on a real review.
- **Mechanism:** **Show the product literally happening.** No b-roll, no graphics — just the actual Tutero experience, captured.
- **Why it won:** The "one-on-one the whole time" line directly contradicts the dominant competitor pattern (group classes, AI chatbots, rotating tutors). It's a wedge.
- **Reusable lesson:** When your competitors compromise on something, the ad that names the compromise wins. "1:1 the whole time" is a wedge against Cluey, Mathspace, Outschool.

### Winner 6 — "Online Tutoring For Prep–Year 12" (mum and teen son, AU flags)

- **Format:** Static, mum with teen son waving at the laptop. Two Australian flag emojis flanking "For Prep–Year 12".
- **Hook:** Headline + flags = instant Australianness signal.
- **Mechanism:** **Local trust marker.** AU flags are doing heavy lifting — they tell the parent "this is not a US/Indian/Filipino tutoring service", which is the second-biggest objection in this market after price.
- **Why it won:** Teen + mum together is the rarest stock setup (most ads use primary-age kids). Signals: we tutor older kids too, and parents stay involved.
- **Reusable lesson:** AU flags, AU accents, AU school terms (Prep, ATAR, NAPLAN, HSC, VCE, QCE) = trust shortcut. Use one per ad minimum.

### Winner 7 — "Online Tutoring For Prep–Year 12" (mum + son, thumbs up, tutor on screen)

- **Format:** Static, mum and primary-school son both giving thumbs-up at the laptop, tutor visible on screen.
- **Hook:** Headline + the thumbs-up itself.
- **Mechanism:** **Triadic proof** — kid happy, parent happy, tutor visible. Three pieces of evidence in one frame.
- **Why it won:** The tutor on screen makes it concrete (vs. a stock laptop shot). The double thumbs-up is ridiculously legible at thumb-stop speed.
- **Reusable lesson:** The strongest static ads contain the **whole transaction in one frame**: child + parent + tutor + laptop. If you can see all four, the ad is doing its job.

### Winner 8 — "On a scale of 1 to 10, where would you rate Tutero?" (UGC video, teen girl)

- **Format:** Vertical UGC video, teen girl in her bedroom, leading question caption.
- **Hook:** A question, not a statement. Stops the scroll because the brain auto-completes it.
- **Mechanism:** **Curiosity gap.** You can't help but want to hear her answer. The teen-in-bedroom setting is the most authentic UGC frame on Meta.
- **Why it won:** Teens reviewing their own tutoring is rare and high-trust — it bypasses the "parent-was-paid-to-say-this" suspicion.
- **Reusable lesson:** Open with a question whose answer the viewer wants. Use the *student* as the reviewer when you can — it's more credible than the parent.

### Winner 9 — "Jess works full-time and uses Tutero for weekly tutoring" (mum + daughter at school gate)

- **Format:** Static, mum in work clothes hugging primary-school daughter in uniform at the school gate. Two chat-bubble overlays: *Jess: Happy mum* / *Ava: Happy in school*.
- **Hook:** "Jess works full-time and uses Tutero for weekly tutoring" — names a specific archetype.
- **Mechanism:** **Persona mirroring.** Working mums see themselves instantly. The chat-bubble device ("Happy mum / Happy in school") is novel and emotional.
- **Why it won:** It's not selling tutoring to parents — it's selling **guilt relief** to working mums. The school gate setting is the exact moment that guilt peaks.
- **Reusable lesson:** Name the persona in the headline ("Jess works full-time…", "Mark's son hated maths…", "Sarah's daughter has ADHD…"). Specificity > breadth.

### Winner 10 — "Tutoring for Prep–Year 12" (tutor avatar starburst)

- **Format:** Static, Tutero logo centred with 8–10 tutor avatars arranged in a sunburst/halo pattern. Subhead: "Our qualified tutors are helping 1000s of school students across Australia. Choose your dream tutor at tutero.com.au".
- **Hook:** Visual hierarchy — eye goes to logo, then to the orbiting tutors, then to "1000s".
- **Mechanism:** **Scale + choice in one image.** "1000s of school students" is the social proof; the avatar halo is the choice signal.
- **Why it won:** It looks like a brand ad, not a performance ad — which paradoxically performs because it pattern-breaks the feed.
- **Reusable lesson:** Once a quarter, run a pure brand-flex ad (logo + scale claim + faces). It builds the brand AND converts the high-intent searchers who already know the name.

### Winner 11 — "Online Tutoring For Prep–Year 12" (3-photo collage)

- **Format:** Static, three real candid photos of mums + kids around laptops, arranged as a tilted polaroid collage. Two ticks below: *Qualified Teachers & Tutors · Personalised 1:1 Lessons*.
- **Hook:** The collage itself — three pieces of proof in one glance.
- **Mechanism:** **Volume of evidence.** One photo = anecdote. Three photos = pattern. The brain reads "lots of families do this".
- **Why it won:** Real (not stock) photos. The polaroid tilt signals "user-submitted", which raises trust.
- **Reusable lesson:** Whenever you have 3+ real customer photos, use them all. A trio of imperfect real photos beats one perfect stock photo every time.

### Winner 12 — "Online Tutoring for Prep–Year 12" (12-tutor grid)

- **Format:** Static, 12 tutor headshots in a 6×2 grid (the maximum density). Five ticked benefits below: *Meticulously Handpicked Tutors · 1:1 Online Lessons · Incredible Resources · Lesson Reports · Engaging Lessons*.
- **Hook:** Sheer density of faces. 12 humans staring at the parent.
- **Mechanism:** **Maximum choice signal.** This is Winner 2 turned up to 11. The benefit list is longer because the brand has earned the right to list more once trust is established by the faces.
- **Why it won:** This is the "abundance" ad — it shows Tutero is a *marketplace*, not a single tutor. Combats the fear "what if the one tutor is bad?".
- **Reusable lesson:** When testing creative variants, always test a **density variant** — same concept, but with 2× the faces. Density itself is a persuasion lever.

## Part 2 — The Seven Recurring Moves

Across all 12 winners, the same seven moves keep appearing. **A winning Tutero AU ad executes at least 3 of these. The strongest winners execute 5+.**

### Move 1 — Year-range clarity in the headline

10 of 12 winners say "Prep–Year 12" or equivalent in the headline.

**Why it works:** It pre-qualifies the click. Parents instantly know "this is for my kid". It also signals breadth — we cover the whole journey, not just one stage.

**Application:** Default to including the year range unless you have a sharper hook. Variants: "Prep–Year 12", "K–12", "Primary & High School", "ATAR-ready tutors".

### Move 2 — Faces, faces, faces

11 of 12 winners feature human faces. The one exception (Winner 1) is a video which is *all* face.

**Why it works:** Faces stop scrolls. Smiling faces signal safety. Multiple faces signal choice and scale.

**Application:** No face = no ad. Minimum one face per static, ideally a real customer or real tutor (not stock). When uncertain between two creatives, pick the one with more faces.

### Move 3 — The risk-reducer tick

The ticked-benefit ads (1, 4, 11, 12) all include at least one tick that removes a parent fear: *Pay Per Lesson · No hidden fees · No lock-in contract · Transparent pricing · Meticulously Handpicked Tutors*.

**Why it works:** Parents are loss-averse. They aren't asking "will this work?" — they're asking "what if this goes wrong?". The risk-reducer answers the second question.

**Application:** Every ticked-benefit ad must have one risk-reducer tick. Place it second in the list (the brain remembers first and last; the risk-reducer should sneak in the middle and feel inevitable).

### Move 4 — Outcome substitution (sell the *real* outcome)

Winner 3 sells confidence + less screen time. Winner 9 sells guilt relief for working mums. Neither sells "better grades".

**Why it works:** Every tutoring ad sells grades. The ones that sell what parents *actually lie awake worrying about* — confidence, screen time, guilt, the relationship at home — break the pattern.

**Application:** Before writing the headline, list the 5 things this parent worries about *that aren't grades*. Pick one. Build the ad around it. Grades can appear as a footnote, never the lead.

### Move 5 — Australianness signal

Winners 6, 9, 10 all carry explicit AU markers (flags, school uniforms, "across Australia", school gate setting). Even Winner 2's tutor names (Rose, Faraz, Zhang, Sarah, Joey, Freya, Sethumi) are deliberately multicultural-Australian.

**Why it works:** The category is flooded with offshore tutoring services. AU parents have been burned. Any unambiguous AU signal is a trust shortcut.

**Application:** Every ad needs at least one AU signal. Options: AU flag, AU school uniform, "Australia" in copy, AU curriculum reference (NAPLAN, ATAR, HSC, VCE, QCE), school gate / Australian home interior, AU accent in voiceover.

### Move 6 — The whole-transaction-in-one-frame

Winner 7 contains child + parent + tutor + laptop in a single static. Winner 5 shows the lesson literally happening. Winner 11 stacks three of these.

**Why it works:** The parent's biggest mental block is "I can't picture how this works". When the ad shows the whole thing happening at once, the imagination work is done for them.

**Application:** For any static where you have access to real customer photos, prioritise the frame that contains the most elements of the transaction simultaneously. Bonus points if the tutor is visible on the screen within the photo.

### Move 7 — Persona specificity

Winner 9 ("Jess works full-time…") names a precise archetype. Winner 8 (teen reviewing her own tutoring) targets teen-girl mums via the messenger.

**Why it works:** "For everyone" sells to no one. Naming a specific person makes everyone *like* that person feel seen, and everyone unlike them ignore it (which is fine — let them).

**Application:** Build a stable of 4-6 persona ads, one per archetype: working mum, ATAR-stressed teen, ADHD/learning-difference parent, "we tried Cluey and it didn't work" switcher, regional/remote family, anxious primary-school parent. Rotate them.

## Part 3 — Pre-Flight Checklist for Any New Ad

Before any new Tutero AU paid social ad ships, run this checklist. **Score 0–7.**

| # | Move | Pass? |
|---|------|-------|
| 1 | Does the headline include a year range OR a sharper hook that earns its place? | ☐ |
| 2 | Is there at least one human face? Ideally real, not stock? | ☐ |
| 3 | If using ticked benefits, is one of them a risk-reducer? | ☐ |
| 4 | Does the ad sell a non-obvious outcome (confidence, screen time, guilt relief, home harmony) instead of just "better grades"? | ☐ |
| 5 | Is there at least one unambiguous Australianness signal? | ☐ |
| 6 | Can the parent see the whole transaction (child + parent + tutor + laptop) OR a substantial chunk of it? | ☐ |
| 7 | Is a specific persona named or visually obvious — not "all parents"? | ☐ |

**Scoring:**
- **0–2:** Don't ship. This ad has no learned pattern in it.
- **3–4:** Ship as experimental variant — creative risk-taking is encouraged. These ads test new territory and may surprise.
- **5–6:** Strong candidate. Likely winner.
- **7:** This is a hero ad. Put real budget behind it.

## Part 4 — Format Heuristics

From the 12 winners, format frequencies:

- **Static with real photo + ticks:** 5 winners (1, 4, 6, 7, 11) — workhorse format
- **Tutor grid / face density:** 3 winners (2, 10, 12) — best for cold audiences
- **UGC video:** 3 winners (1, 5, 8) — best for warm audiences and curiosity-led hooks
- **Testimonial portrait:** 1 winner (3) — best for retargeting

**Default mix for a new test cohort:**
- 2× workhorse statics (real photo + 3 ticks)
- 1× tutor grid (cold audience)
- 1× UGC video (curiosity hook)
- 1× testimonial portrait (retargeting)

## Part 5 — Anti-Patterns (What the Winners Never Do)

None of the 12 winners do any of the following. If a draft contains these, fix it before shipping.

1. **Generic stock photos with no AU markers.** Every winner has a real or AU-coded image.
2. **"AI-powered" language.** Zero winners mention AI in the ad. (See `tutero-tutoring-parent-copy-australia` for the forbidden words list.)
3. **Long copy blocks.** Every winner is scannable in under 2 seconds.
4. **Single benefit walls of text.** When benefits appear, they appear as 3-5 ticks max.
5. **Founder-y messaging.** No "we built this because…" stories. Parents don't care about the founder narrative in a paid ad.
6. **Discount-led hooks.** No "$X off" or "free trial" leading. Risk-reducers (no lock-in, pay per lesson) work; discounts cheapen the brand.
7. **Faceless brand ads.** Even Winner 10, the most brand-heavy, is built around tutor avatars.

## Part 6 — How to Use This Skill

**When briefing a new ad:**
1. Identify which persona you're targeting (Move 7).
2. Pick the format (Part 4).
3. Draft the headline using Moves 1, 4, or 7.
4. Build the visual using Moves 2, 5, 6.
5. If using ticks, apply Move 3.
6. Run the Part 3 checklist. If <3, rework. Scores of 3–4 are acceptable for experimental variants.

**When critiquing an existing ad:**
1. Run the Part 3 checklist first. Score it.
2. Identify which moves it's missing.
3. Suggest the smallest change that adds the highest-value missing move (usually Move 4 or Move 7 — outcome substitution and persona specificity are the most often skipped).

**When choosing between two creative options:**
- Higher checklist score wins. Tiebreaker: more faces.
