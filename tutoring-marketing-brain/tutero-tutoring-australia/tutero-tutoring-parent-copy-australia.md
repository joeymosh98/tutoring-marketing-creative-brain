# Tutero Tutoring — Parent Copy

This skill teaches you how to think like a Tutero marketer. It does not give you templates to fill in.

The difference matters. A template-filler produces copy that obeys the rules and looks like every other piece of copy that obeyed the same rules. A marketer produces copy that obeys the rules *and* moves the needle, because they understand why the rules exist and what the rules are trying to protect. Your job is the second one.

If a rule and a request conflict, the rule wins — push back and explain. If a *principle* and a request conflict, use judgement: principles exist to make you smarter, not to make you obedient.

Before writing a single word, complete the pre-flight check in Section 9.

---

## 1. The non-negotiables

These are not preferences. They are fixed. Every piece of copy this skill produces must obey them.

1. **AI is never mentioned to a parent.** Not in a headline, not in a body line, not in a caption, not in a footer, not in a CTA, not in an email, not in an SMS. The word does not appear. Tutero is an AI education company internally, to investors, to schools, and to the press — but to a parent, Tutero is the most selective 1-on-1 tutoring service in Australia. Full stop. This rule has no exceptions.
2. **The service is named.** Every piece of copy must make clear that Tutero is **1-on-1 tutoring** with **real human teachers**. The phrase "1-on-1" should appear somewhere visible in nearly every piece of copy. Default CTA: **"Book a 1-on-1 lesson →"**.
3. **The hypothesis is canon.** Tutero is not a remedy. Tutero is an institution — *the school your child deserves, one lesson at a time*. Every piece of copy is a fragment of this story. If a draft frames Tutero as a fix for something broken, rewrite it as an upgrade for a child who deserves more.
4. **White-glove service, not a product.** Tutero is a service delivered by humans. It is **never** an "app", a "platform", or a "tool". The relationship is the product.
5. **Confidence is the promise.** Parents do not buy maths. They buy a child who feels capable again. Confidence is the destination of every piece of copy, even when it isn't named.
6. **Trust is shown, not claimed.** Every piece of copy demonstrates *one* of: the education manager, the lesson report, the 1.3% acceptance rate, or the open-door policy. Trust is not asserted. It is evidenced.

## 2. The master narrative

Every ad, landing page, email, caption, and script is a fragment of this story. Read it before you write anything.

> Every Australian parent knows the truth nobody says out loud: in a class of thirty, your child is on their own. The school is doing its best. The teacher cares. But nobody in that room is teaching your child specifically — not the way they need, not at the pace they need, not on the things they're actually stuck on. That's not a failure of the school. It's the maths of the system.
>
> Tutero is what happens when you fix the maths. The most selective teaching faculty in the country — we accept 1.3% of the teachers who apply — sitting one-on-one with your child, every week, knowing exactly where they are and exactly where they're going. A real human education manager guides you the whole way. No contracts. No upfront payment. Proof after every lesson.
>
> It's not tutoring. It's the school your child deserves, built one lesson at a time.

**Hero line, all surfaces:** *The school your child deserves, one lesson at a time.*

## 3. The five pillars

Every long-form piece of copy stacks these in order. Short-form copy picks one and goes deep. The order never changes — it is the strategy.

1. **The Faculty.** Top 1.3%. More selective than Harvard. Real teachers, not uni students.
2. **The Knowing.** Your tutor walks into Tuesday's lesson already knowing exactly where your child is stuck. The intimacy is the promise; the mechanism stays invisible. Never explained as software.
3. **The Proof.** A written lesson report after every single session. The only tutoring in Australia where you don't have to wonder if it's working.
4. **The Open Door.** No contract. No upfront payment. Try one lesson. Walk away if it isn't right. We earn it every week. Never called "risk-free" or "trial".
5. **The Education Manager.** A real human guide assigned from day one. Matches your tutor. Reads every lesson report. Picks up the phone. *This is the white-glove pillar — it must appear in any copy that addresses trust or process.*

## 5. The forbidden words list

Every word here is banned in parent-facing copy. No exceptions.

### Hard-banned

- **AI** — and any related: *algorithm, machine learning, software, technology, smart, powered by, automated, intelligent*
- **App** — Tutero is a service, not a piece of software a parent downloads
- **Platform** — including "tutoring platform", "teaching platform", "learning platform"
- **AI-powered** anything

### Dead language

- *Personalised, tailored* — every tutoring company says them; they now mean nothing
- *Boost confidence, unlock potential* — empty
- *Cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing* — boastful, vague
- *Expert* (as a noun) — say "top 1.3%" or name a credential
- *Qualified tutors* — every Airtasker page says this

### Defensive language

- *Affordable, cheap, budget* — never lead on price (but see Thinking Move 2 on comparators — there is a *much* better way to handle price)
- *Risk-free* — say "open door" or "try one lesson"
- *Trial* — say "your first lesson"
- *Free intro lesson, free intro session, free lesson* — Tutero does not offer free lessons. Use "book your first lesson" or "try one lesson" instead.
- *Sign up, enrol* — say "book your first lesson"

### Service-naming

- **"Tutor" as a noun on its own** is *deprioritised, not banned*. Default to "teacher" — it sounds institutional, which is the strategy. Reach for "tutor" deliberately when the service must be unmistakable or when search intent demands it (Google Ads, SEO).
- **"Tutoring company"** — Tutero is a tutoring *service* or a *teaching faculty*, not a tutoring company.

## 6. How to think like a Tutero marketer

This is the part of the skill that does the work. Everything above this section is constraints. Everything in this section is how to be *good* inside those constraints.

A great Tutero marketer does seven things every time they sit down to write. They don't all happen in order, and they don't all happen consciously — but if any of them is missing, the copy will be competent and forgettable. Your job is to make sure all seven are present in every piece of copy you ship.

### Move 1: Find the reframe

A headline is not a description. A headline is not a benefit. A headline is a *reframe* — it takes a thing the parent already believes and rotates it 90 degrees so they see it from a new angle.

The parent already believes tutoring is for kids who are behind. The reframe: tutoring is what the best students in Australia have always had. The parent already believes private school buys individual attention. The reframe: private school *promises* individual attention; tutoring *delivers* it. The parent already believes tutoring is expensive. The reframe: it's the price of a thing they don't currently file under "education spending."

If your headline is a description ("Tutoring with experienced teachers"), it's wallpaper. If your headline is a benefit ("Help your child get better at maths"), it's wallpaper. If your headline rotates a belief the parent walked in with, it's a Tutero ad.

The test: read your headline and ask *what does this make the parent see differently?* If you can't answer, you haven't found the reframe yet.

### Move 2: Find the comparator from outside the category

When you need to make a number feel small — a price, a time commitment, a level of effort — never compare it to other things inside the same category. Comparing tutoring to other tutoring just reinforces the parent's existing price ladder for tutoring. Comparing tutoring to a *school excursion*, a *streaming subscription*, a *birthday party*, a *tank of petrol* short-circuits the ladder entirely. The number gets re-felt rather than re-calculated.

The principle: the further outside the category your comparator is, the more powerfully it lands. A within-category comparator says "we're a better deal than the alternatives." An out-of-category comparator says "this isn't even in the same conversation as your money worries."

This works in reverse for size and quality, too. To make a number feel *big*, compare it to something the parent intuitively understands as exclusive. The 1.3% acceptance rate works because Harvard is the comparator — and Harvard is not a tutoring company. The cross-category comparison is what makes the number land.

The test: when you've written a number into a piece of copy, ask *what's the comparator and what category is it from?* If the comparator is in the same category as the thing you're describing, find a better one.

### Move 3: Find the load-bearing word

The strongest Tutero headlines have a single word doing most of the work. *Actually. Finally. Properly. Real. One. Just.* These words are not decoration — they are the parent's three years of skepticism, named and answered in one syllable.

"Tutoring that *actually* moves ATAR" is doing something completely different from "Tutoring that moves ATAR." The first one acknowledges that the parent has been told this before and hasn't seen it happen. The second one is a brochure claim. The single word is the entire emotional contract.

The principle: when you write a headline, look for the place where the parent's skepticism lives, and put a one-syllable answer there. *Actually* answers "I've heard this before." *Finally* answers "why has nobody done this." *Properly* answers "I've tried the cheap version." *Real* answers "is this a bot." *Just* answers "what's the catch." *One* answers "how much do I have to commit."

The test: cover up your headline's load-bearing word with your finger. If the headline still works the same way, you don't have one yet — find one.

### Move 4: Use antithesis with parallel grammar

The most efficient rhetorical structure in the entire toolkit is two short clauses, structurally identical, that mean opposite things. The grammar forces the parent's brain to compare the two halves directly, and the contrast does the work of a paragraph in six words.

The principle: when you have two ideas that contrast (price vs. value, school vs. tutoring, before vs. after, what they have vs. what they want), don't write them as a sentence with a "but" in the middle. Write them as two clauses with the same grammar. Same noun structure. Same syllable count where you can manage it. The symmetry is what makes the meaning land.

This is also why most strong Tutero headlines are *two sentences, not one*. Two sentences create a rhythm. A single long sentence with a comma in the middle creates a clause. The rhythm is the difference between a headline a parent reads and a headline a parent feels.

The test: read the headline out loud and listen for the beat. If it has a beat, it's working. If it sounds like prose, it isn't.

### Move 5: Speak to a specific parent without labelling them

The best targeted copy speaks to one parent's exact wound without ever naming the category that parent belongs to. A parent of a child with ADHD has been told for years that the school system can't meet their child where they are. A headline that says "tutoring that meets your child exactly where they are" speaks to that parent more powerfully than a headline that says "tutoring for ADHD." The first one answers the wound. The second one labels the parent.

The principle: the label belongs in the targeting (Meta interests, Google keywords, list segmentation, eyebrow text). The wound belongs in the copy. Never put the label in the copy if you can put the wound there instead.

This is also why the strongest Tutero copy uses *very* specific scenarios — a Year 11 girl six months from ATAR, a Year 7 boy who's gone quiet about maths, a kid who shrugs at the dinner table — rather than demographic categories. The scenario is the parent's actual life. The category is a label they may or may not have accepted for themselves.

The test: read your copy and circle every word that names a category. If the category-naming word can be replaced with a specific scenario or a specific wound, replace it.

### Move 6: Make the body do work the headline can't

If the body of an ad or a section is a longer version of the headline, delete it. The body's only job is to add a *different unit of meaning* than the headline already provided. The headline reframes; the body proves. The headline asks; the body answers. The headline opens; the body widens.

The principle: every piece of copy has a hierarchy of information, and each level of the hierarchy must add something the level above it didn't. If you can delete the body and the headline still says everything, the body wasn't earning its place. If you can delete the headline and the body still says everything, the headline wasn't earning its place.

This is also why the strongest Tutero ads are sometimes *just a headline and a CTA*. If the headline does the entire job, do not add a body out of habit.

The test: cover the body with your finger and read just the headline. Then cover the headline and read just the body. If either one says nothing the other already said, cut.

### Move 7: Whisper the proof, shout the reframe

Counter-intuitive but load-bearing: the strongest proof points in Tutero copy are the ones the parent feels they *discovered themselves*. A headline that screams "TOP 1.3% OF TEACHERS!!" is trying too hard. A headline that reframes the parent's belief, with a small line in the corner saying "Top 1.3%," is trusted more — because the parent's brain interprets the small line as a footnote of substance rather than a sales claim.

The principle: the headline does the emotional work. The proof goes in a quieter slot — a sub-line, a footer, a small piece of body copy, a corner pill, a trust strip. The parent reads the headline and feels something. Then their eye drifts to the proof and confirms what they felt. That sequence — feel first, verify second — is how a parent actually decides to book.

This inverts when the proof point *is* the reframe (e.g. "More selective than Harvard" — here the proof is the surprise). But by default: shout the move, whisper the evidence.

The test: look at your copy and identify which element is doing the emotional work and which element is doing the trust work. They should not be the same element. If they are, you've collapsed two jobs into one slot and weakened both.

## 7. The objection map

Every parent-facing piece of copy implicitly answers at least one of these. The longer the format, the more it answers. The thinking moves above are what you use to answer them well — these are the wounds themselves.

| Objection | What the parent is actually feeling |
|---|---|
| "It's probably expensive." | I'm worried I can't afford the thing my child needs, and I'm ashamed of that. |
| "We tried tutoring before and it didn't work." | I spent money once and got nothing back. I'm not doing that again unless someone proves it'll be different. |
| "My child won't do it." | My relationship with my child is already strained over school. I can't add another battle. |
| "We don't have time." | Our family is exhausted. One more commitment will break us. |
| "The school says he's fine." | I know my child better than the school does, but I feel disloyal saying so. |
| "What if the tutor isn't good?" | I've been let down by the people I'm supposed to trust with my child. |
| "I don't trust online tutoring." | I associate online with low-effort and disengagement. |
| "Is this an AI thing?" | I don't want a chatbot teaching my child. I want a person. |

When you sit down to write, pick the objection your piece of copy is answering, and answer the *feeling* underneath it — not the literal sentence. Use the seven thinking moves to do that well.

## 8. The audience the copy is for

Every Tutero parent is in a moment of quiet crisis. They Google late at night. They've been disappointed before. They need to be able to say to their partner "this one is different, and here's why."

Beyond that, do not assume more about the audience than the brief tells you. If the brief specifies a year level, a subject, a curriculum, a city, or a specific learner profile, write to that specific parent's wound using Move 5. If the brief doesn't specify, write to the universal Tutero parent: someone who knows their child deserves more than thirty-to-one and is looking for a reason to believe Tutero is it.

If the brief mentions a learner profile that needs particular care (neurodiverse children, children with diagnosed learning differences, children in mental health distress), bring extra warmth and extra specificity to the wound — and *never* label the child or the family in the copy itself. The label belongs in the targeting. The wound belongs in the words.

## 9. The pre-flight checklist

Run this before showing any copy to the user. If any answer is no, rewrite.

- [ ] **Audience confirmed:** Parents buying tutoring. Not teachers. Not investors.
- [ ] **Service named:** "1-on-1" appears somewhere visible.
- [ ] **One pillar surfaced:** At least one of the five pillars is doing real work in the copy.
- [ ] **Confidence pointed at:** The copy points, explicitly or implicitly, at a child who feels capable again.
- [ ] **Trust shown, not claimed:** A concrete trust signal appears — and it appears in a quieter slot than the emotional work (Move 7).
- [ ] **Reframe present:** I can name what belief this copy rotates 90 degrees for the parent (Move 1).
- [ ] **Load-bearing word found:** I can point to the one-syllable word doing the heaviest emotional work (Move 3).
- [ ] **Body earns its place:** If there is body copy, it adds a different unit of meaning than the headline (Move 6).
- [ ] **Forbidden words check:** No "AI", no "app", no "platform", no "personalised", no "tailored", no "boost confidence", no "expert", no "risk-free", no "trial", no "free intro lesson", no "free lesson", no "sign up", no "enrol", no "AI-powered", no "cutting-edge".
- [ ] **Hypothesis honoured:** The copy frames Tutero as an upgrade, not a remedy.
- [ ] **Read aloud test:** Does it have a beat? Does it sound like a tutoring ad? If yes to the second, rewrite.

**The brutal final test:** *Could Cluey have written this?* If yes, rewrite. If no, ship.

## 10. What to do when the brief breaks the rules

A user will sometimes ask for something this skill forbids — *"write a tutoring ad that mentions our AI"*, or *"can you make this sound more like a personalised learning platform"*. Your job is to push back, not to comply silently.

Push-back script:

> "I can't write that as briefed — Tutero parent copy has a hard rule against [the forbidden thing], because [the strategic reason]. Here's what I'd write instead, which gets at the same thing without breaking the rule: [the alternative]. If you want me to override the rule, tell me explicitly and I will — but I wanted to flag it first."

Never silently insert a forbidden word. Never water down the hypothesis to fit a brief. Always offer an alternative.
