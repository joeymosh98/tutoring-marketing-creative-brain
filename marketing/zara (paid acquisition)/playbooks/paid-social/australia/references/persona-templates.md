# Persona Templates

Visual reference guide for the parent types and sub-segments defined in `creating-paid-social-ads` (Step 2). Use these when generating images — they give `image-generator` concrete visual detail for each parent type.

**Important:** These are visual *guidance*, not rigid templates. The parent type and reason to buy (from the cognition system) drive creative decisions. These descriptions help you picture the person in the frame.

---

## The Three Parent Types

### 1. The Worried Parent

**Feeling:** Sees their child struggling, feels helpless, needs reassurance that someone competent will step in.
**Reasons to buy served:** Child is falling behind (1), child's confidence is low (5), emotional support and mentorship (7).
**Best templates:** B (Text-on-Photo), H (Confidence Statement), K (Story Card).

**Visual guidance:**
- Age: 35–48
- Expression: warm but carrying visible concern — the look of a parent who's been thinking about this for a while
- Clothing: casual home — soft jumper, comfortable shirt, nothing formal
- Setting: kitchen table, family room couch, or sitting beside the child at a desk — domestic and real
- Child: any age, ideally shown engaged with a tutor on screen (the visual proof that it's working)
- Emotional beat: the moment the worry starts to lift — parent watching their child actually get it
- AU signals: Aussie suburban home, school uniform if child is school-age

---

### 2. The Ambitious Parent

**Feeling:** Wants to push their child to reach their potential, sees tutoring as an investment.
**Reasons to buy served:** Upcoming exams (4), accountability (6), unsure how to help their child improve (2).
**Best templates:** A (Workhorse), G (50/50 Split), J (Tutor Spotlight).

**Visual guidance:**
- Age: 38–50
- Expression: focused, purposeful — this parent is solving a problem, not drowning in one
- Clothing: slightly sharper casual — a clean shirt, a blazer over a t-shirt, smart-casual work clothes
- Setting: dining table with textbooks, a well-lit study area, or kitchen counter — organised, not chaotic
- Child: often a teen (14–18), in school uniform or casual home clothes, studying or on a laptop
- Emotional beat: parent and child working *with* each other, not against — the rare moment the household stress is gone
- AU signals: ATAR/HSC/VCE study notes visible, Year 11/12 textbook, AU school uniform

---

### 3. The Frustrated Parent

**Feeling:** The school system isn't doing enough. Looking for something better.
**Reasons to buy served:** School system isn't supporting their child (3), unsure how to help (2), role model (8).
**Best templates:** D (Quote Card), H (Confidence Statement), B (Text-on-Photo).

**Visual guidance:**
- Age: 35–48
- Expression: determined, slightly over it — the body language of someone who's done waiting for the school to fix it
- Clothing: casual, practical — this parent is busy and direct
- Setting: any normal Australian home — kitchen, family room
- Child: any age, ideally shown thriving with the tutor (the visual counterpoint to the school frustration)
- Emotional beat: quiet relief — "we finally found something that works"
- AU signals: standard suburban AU home, school bag or uniform in the background

## Sub-Segments

Use these for targeting and for adding specificity when a broad type isn't enough. Each sub-segment sits within one or more of the three parent types above.

### Switchers (post-Cluey, post-tutoring-centre)

**Parent type:** Frustrated parent (primarily).
**Sells:** "We tried the others and they didn't work. Tutero is different." Highest-intent segment — they've already paid for tutoring elsewhere.
**Best templates:** D (Quote Card), H (Confidence Statement), B (Text-on-Photo).

**Visual guidance:**
- Mood: relieved, slightly amused — the body language of someone who's been through a few attempts and finally found the one that works
- Setting: the most "normal" possible Australian home — this segment converts on *comfort*
- Child: any age, visibly engaged

**Visual rule:** Never name a competitor in the image. Keep the visual clean and positive. The competitor framing lives in the headline copy only.

---

### Special Learning Needs (ADHD, learning differences)

**Parent type:** Worried parent (primarily).
**Sells:** Patience. 1:1 attention. The fact that the child won't be left behind or made to feel stupid.
**Best templates:** D (Quote Card), H (Confidence Statement), K (Story Card).

**This sub-segment requires extra care.** The image must NEVER signal pity, struggle, or difference in a way that could feel exploitative. The child should look engaged and happy — the *parent* in the copy carries the weight of the learning-needs framing, not the visual.

**Visual guidance:**
- Child: 8–12, in casual home clothes (NOT school uniform — implies a more flexible learning approach)
- Setting: family room, kitchen, or a calm corner of the home with soft furnishings
- Emotional beat: child is actually engaged with the tutor on screen, not zoning out — this is the visual proof

**Avoid in the image:** Anything that visually flags the child as "different" or "struggling". The sub-segment is named in the headline copy — the visual is just a happy kid getting good 1:1 attention.

---

### Regional Families

**Parent type:** Frustrated parent or worried parent.
**Sells:** Access. "Same quality as a Sydney/Melbourne kid". No driving 90 minutes for a tutor.
**Best templates:** B (Text-on-Photo), G (50/50 Split), F (Polaroid Collage).

**Visual guidance:**
- Clothing: practical, regional Australian — bootcut jeans, work shirt, riding boots, Akubra-adjacent hat hanging on a hook in the background
- Setting: rural-coded Australian home — wooden farmhouse kitchen, country veranda visible through a window, paddock outside
- Child: any age, often in a regional/rural school uniform (bottle-green or maroon rather than suburban navy)
- Emotional beat: the parent's quiet pride that their kid has access
- AU signals: this is the strongest possible AU signal — visibly regional Australia

---

### Busy Working Parents

**Parent type:** Worried parent or ambitious parent.
**Sells:** Guilt relief. Convenience. "I can't be everywhere."
**Best templates:** A (Workhorse), B (Text-on-Photo), G (50/50 Split).

**Visual guidance:**
- Clothing: smart casual work — blazer over t-shirt, professional dress, lanyard sometimes visible
- Setting: school gate, kitchen counter on a weekday morning, or coming home through the front door with a laptop bag still on her shoulder
- Child: primary-school age (6–11), in school uniform
- Emotional beat: the moment they relax because they know their kid has tutoring sorted

## How to Use These in the Pipeline

These visual descriptions feed into image generation, not into copy or template selection. The workflow is:

1. Run the cognition system in `creating-paid-social-ads` — identify reason to buy, parent type, template, and copy approach
2. When generating images via `image-generator`, use the visual guidance here to describe the parent and child in the scene
3. Append the anti-AI-glow rules from `aesthetic-rules`
4. Adapt freely — these are starting points, not scripts. Mix details across sub-segments when it serves the ad

Always rotate parent types across a campaign — at least 2–3 different types live at any time, refreshing the lowest-performer monthly.
