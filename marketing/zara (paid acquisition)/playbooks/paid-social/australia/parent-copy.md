# Tutero Tutoring — Parent Copy

This skill teaches you how to write copy that sells tutoring to Australian parents.

The single most important thing: **clarity sells.** When the headline clearly names the product and who it's for, people purchase. There is real performance data behind this — clear headlines like "1-on-1 Tutoring for Prep – Year 12" consistently outperform clever reframes. Wordplay and rhetorical moves are optional enhancements. They never come at the cost of clarity.

If a rule and a request conflict, the rule wins — push back and explain. If a *principle* and a request conflict, use judgement.

Before writing a single word, complete the pre-flight check in Section 10.

---

## 1. The non-negotiables

These are fixed. Every piece of copy must obey them.

1. **Clarity is the #1 job.** A parent scrolling at speed must understand in 2 seconds: (a) what the product is and (b) who it's for. "1-on-1 Tutoring" + "for Prep – Year 12" performs better than any clever reframe. When in doubt between clear and clever, choose clear. Always.
2. **AI is never mentioned to a parent.** Not in a headline, body, caption, footer, CTA, email, or SMS. Tutero is an AI education company internally, to investors, and to schools — but to a parent, Tutero is the most selective 1-on-1 tutoring service in Australia. This rule has no exceptions.
3. **The service is named.** Every piece of copy must make clear that Tutero is **1-on-1 tutoring** with **real humans**. The phrase "1-on-1" should appear somewhere visible. Default CTA: **"Book a 1-on-1 lesson →"**.
4. **Use "tutor" as the default noun.** Not all Tutero tutors are qualified teachers — many are qualified tutors. Say "tutor" unless you know the specific person is a registered teacher. Never say "all of our tutors are teachers." "Teacher" is fine when referring to a specific tutor who IS a teacher, but "tutor" is the safe, accurate default.
5. **List what you do, then who it's for.** The proven headline pattern: clear product name in the hero, clear audience in the subhead. "Online Tutoring" + "for Prep – Year 12 🇦🇺" is the structure that converts. Don't bury the product behind a clever opening.
6. **Confidence is the promise.** Parents don't buy maths. They buy a child who feels capable again. Confidence is the destination of every piece of copy, even when it isn't named directly.
7. **Trust is shown, not claimed.** Every piece of copy demonstrates *one* of: the personal account manager, the lesson report, the 1.3% acceptance rate, the money back guarantee, or vetted & verified tutors. Trust is not asserted — it is evidenced.
8. **White-glove service, not a product.** Tutero is a service delivered by humans. It is **never** an "app", a "platform", or a "tool". The relationship is the product.

## 2. The master narrative

Every ad, landing page, email, caption, and script is a fragment of this story. Read it before you write anything.

> Every Australian parent knows the truth nobody says out loud: in a class of thirty, your child is on their own. The school is doing its best. The teacher cares. But nobody in that room is teaching your child specifically — not the way they need, not at the pace they need, not on the things they're actually stuck on. That's not a failure of the school. It's the maths of the system.
>
> Tutero is what happens when you fix the maths. A carefully matched tutor — vetted, verified, and from the top 1.3% of applicants — sitting one-on-one with your child, every week, knowing exactly where they are and exactly where they're going. A personal account manager guides you the whole way. No contracts. No upfront fees. Cancel anytime.
>
> It's not tutoring. It's the school your child deserves, built one lesson at a time.

**Hero line, all surfaces:** *The school your child deserves, one lesson at a time.*

## 3. The five pillars

Every long-form piece of copy stacks these in order. Short-form copy picks one and goes deep. The order never changes.

1. **The Faculty.** Top 1.3% acceptance rate. Vetted and verified. Custom tutor pairing — matched by a real person, not an algorithm.
2. **The Knowing.** Your tutor walks into Tuesday's lesson already knowing where your child is stuck. We find and fix gaps in knowledge. Weekly lesson reports so you always know what's happening.
3. **The Proof.** A written lesson report after every session. Watch your child's confidence grow. Improve grades. See progress after 3 lessons.
4. **The Open Door.** No contracts. No upfront fees. Cancel anytime. Money back guarantee. Free tutoring consultation to get started.
5. **The Account Manager.** A real person assigned from day one. Matches your tutor. Reads every lesson report. Picks up the phone. *This is the white-glove pillar — it must appear in any copy that addresses trust or process.*

## 5. The forbidden words list

### Hard-banned

- **AI** — and any related: *algorithm, machine learning, software, technology, smart, powered by, automated, intelligent*
- **Platform** — including "tutoring platform", "teaching platform", "learning platform"
- **AI-powered** anything
- **App** as a product descriptor — Tutero is a service, not an app. However, mentioning the "mobile app for parents" as a *feature* is fine (e.g., as a tick).

### Dead language

- *Cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing* — boastful, vague
- *Expert* (as a noun) — say "top 1.3%" or name a credential
- *Unlock potential* — empty
- *Tailored* — too vague, prefer specific descriptions of what the customisation actually is

### Previously banned, now approved

Real-world ad performance shows these words work when used well:

- **Personalised** — ✅ approved. "Personalised tutoring for your child" is clear and effective.
- **Qualified tutors** — ✅ approved. Accurate — not all tutors are teachers. "Vetted & verified tutors" is even stronger.
- **Affordable** — ✅ approved as a benefit tick. "Affordable pricing" works. Still don't lead with price in headlines.
- **Grow confidence / confidence soar** — ✅ approved. Direct confidence language works. ("Boost confidence" remains weak — prefer "grow confidence" or "watch your child's confidence soar.")

### Defensive language (reframe, don't use directly)

- *Risk-free* — say "no contracts" or "money back guarantee" or "cancel anytime"
- *Trial* — say "your first lesson" or "free tutoring consultation"
- *Free intro lesson, free session, free lesson* — Tutero does not offer free lessons. Use "free tutoring consultation" or "book your first lesson."
- *Sign up, enrol* — say "book your first lesson" or "get started"
- *Cheap, budget* — use "affordable pricing" as a tick if needed

### Service-naming

- **"Tutor"** is the default noun. Accurate for all Tutero tutors.
- **"Teacher"** is fine when the specific person IS a qualified teacher, but never as a blanket term for all tutors.
- **"Tutoring company"** — say tutoring *service*.

## 6. How to think like a Tutero marketer

Everything above this section is constraints. This section is how to be *good* inside those constraints.

There are five thinking moves. **Move 1 is mandatory. Moves 2–5 are optional enhancements.** If you only do one thing, do Move 1 well.

### Move 1: Lead with clarity (MANDATORY)

The headline must answer two questions instantly: **What is this?** and **Who is this for?**

The proven pattern: name the product in the hero, name the audience in the subhead.

**Headlines that work:**
- "1-on-1 Tutoring" / "for Prep – Year 12 🇦🇺"
- "Online Tutoring" / "for Prep – Year 12 🇦🇺"
- "Private Tutoring" / "for Prep – Year 12"
- "Personalised Tutoring for Your Child"
- "Online Tutoring for Australian Students"
- "Our tutors would love to help your child this term."
- "Grow your child's confidence *this term.*"

**Headlines that don't work:**
- "She made one phone call you haven't" — clever, but a scrolling parent has no idea what you're selling
- "Less worry. More Anna." — who is Anna? What is this for?
- "Meet your teacher before you commit" — not all tutors are teachers, and it's unclear what the product is
- "What one mum said after week three" — vague, doesn't name the product

**The test:** Show the headline to someone who has never heard of Tutero. In 2 seconds, can they tell you (a) it's tutoring and (b) who it's for? If not, rewrite.

This doesn't mean every headline must literally say "1-on-1 Tutoring." It means the product and audience must be unmistakably clear — either in the headline itself or through the headline + subhead combination.

### Move 2: Stack specific benefits

The best-performing ads list concrete things the parent gets. Not one abstract benefit — multiple specific ones. The ticks in Tutero ads are not filler; they're the reason parents click.

Draw from the approved copy bank (Section 7). Every tick should answer the question: "What do I actually get?"

**Strong benefit stacks:**
- Same Tutor Every Week + Weekly Lesson Reports + No Contracts
- Vetted & Verified Tutors + Fun & Engaging Lessons + Cancel Anytime
- Aligned to Curriculum + Any Subject + Money Back Guarantee

**Weak benefit stacks:**
- Generic + Generic + Generic ("Great tutors + Great lessons + Great outcomes")
- All from the same category (three cost benefits with no quality signal)

Always mix categories in a 3-tick stack. Ideal pattern:
1. **Service quality tick** (what makes Tutero special)
2. **Risk-reducer tick** (why it's safe to try)
3. **Outcome or convenience tick** (what the parent gets)

The middle position should be the risk-reducer (no contracts, cancel anytime, money back guarantee). Parents are loss-averse — the risk-reducer is the tick that converts.

### Move 3: Write for the feeling underneath the objection

Every parent has an objection they haven't spoken out loud. Great copy answers the *feeling*, not the literal words. See the Objection Map (Section 8) for the full list.

Specificity matters. Don't write to "all parents." Write to one parent's exact wound without labelling them. A parent of a child with ADHD responds more powerfully to "tutoring that meets your child exactly where they are" than "tutoring for ADHD." The wound belongs in the copy. The label belongs in the targeting (Meta interests, audience segments).

### Move 4: Use plain language and rhythm

Short sentences. Plain Australian English. Read it out loud.

Two short sentences create a rhythm. One long sentence with three commas creates a slog. The rhythm is the difference between a headline a parent reads and a headline a parent skips.

Antithesis (two balanced clauses that contrast) is powerful when you need it: "30 kids in a class. 1 tutor who knows yours." But don't force it — clarity first, rhythm second.

### Move 5: Put proof in the quiet slots

The strongest proof points are the ones the parent feels they *discovered*. A headline that screams "TOP 1.3%!!" is trying too hard. A clear headline with a quiet "1.3% acceptance rate" in a sub-line or tick is trusted more — the parent reads the headline and understands the product, then their eye drifts to the proof and it confirms their interest.

The headline does the clarity work. The proof goes in a quieter slot: sub-line, tick, footer pill, trust strip.

Exception: when the proof IS the headline (e.g., "We accept 1.3% of tutors who apply"). This works because the stat itself is clear and specific about what the service delivers.

### Two registers of copy

The items above are **tick copy** — short benefit pills for on-image ticks. They're blunt, scannable, and factual. But Tutero also has a second register: **strategic messaging lines**. These are fuller sentences that do emotional and narrative work. They belong in primary text (Meta ad copy above the image), subheads, body copy, supporting lines (Template H), and story cards (Template K).

The difference:
- **Tick copy:** "Same Tutor Every Week" — states a fact
- **Strategic line:** "She will build a partnership with her tutor." — paints a picture of what that fact *feels like*

Both registers are useful. Ticks go on the image. Strategic lines go in the surrounding copy. Never put a strategic line in a tick (too long), and never use a tick as body copy (too blunt).

### Strategic messaging lines

These are pre-approved narrative sentences. Use them in primary text, subheads, body copy, and supporting lines. Adapt the pronouns (he/she/they/your child) to match the persona.

**Relationship & trust:**
- She will build a partnership with her tutor.
- Our tutors assess how each student learns best.
- A private tutor for your child.
- 1:1 lessons, always.

**Confidence & outcomes:**
- Her confidence will grow every lesson.
- We repair gaps that block confidence.
- Watch something shift in the first few weeks.

**Risk & commitment:**
- Try a few lessons without any risk.
- A great investment into their future.

**Value & comparison:**
- Much cheaper than private school.
- The attention of a private school, without the price tag.

**How to write new strategic lines:**
1. Pick a tick from the copy bank (e.g., "Same Tutor Every Week")
2. Ask: *what does this feel like for the parent or child?*
3. Write a sentence that answers that feeling, not the feature
4. Keep it under 12 words. One idea per line. Plain language.

Strategic lines should feel like something a friend would say to another parent — warm, specific, and grounded. If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it as a conversation.

## 8. The objection map

Every parent-facing piece of copy implicitly answers at least one of these. The longer the format, the more it answers.

| Objection | What the parent is actually feeling |
|---|---|
| "It's probably expensive." | I'm worried I can't afford the thing my child needs. |
| "We tried tutoring before and it didn't work." | I spent money once and got nothing back. |
| "My child won't do it." | My relationship with my child is already strained over school. |
| "We don't have time." | Our family is exhausted. One more commitment will break us. |
| "The school says he's fine." | I know my child better than the school does. |
| "What if the tutor isn't good?" | I've been let down by people I trusted with my child. |
| "I don't trust online tutoring." | I associate online with low-effort and disengagement. |
| "Is this an AI thing?" | I don't want a chatbot teaching my child. I want a person. |

When you sit down to write, pick the objection your piece of copy is answering, and answer the *feeling* — not the literal sentence.

## 9. The audience

Every Tutero parent is in a moment of quiet crisis. They Google late at night. They've been disappointed before. They need to be able to say to their partner "this one is different, and here's why."

If the brief specifies a year level, subject, curriculum, city, or learner profile, write to that specific parent using Move 3. If the brief doesn't specify, write to the universal Tutero parent: someone who knows their child deserves more than thirty-to-one.

If the brief mentions a learner profile that needs particular care (neurodiverse children, children with diagnosed learning differences), bring extra warmth and specificity to the wound — and *never* label the child or the family in the copy. The label belongs in the targeting. The wound belongs in the words.

## 10. The pre-flight checklist

Run this before showing any copy. If any answer is no, rewrite.

- [ ] **Clarity first:** A parent scrolling in 2 seconds knows this is tutoring and who it's for.
- [ ] **Service named:** "1-on-1" or "tutoring" appears somewhere visible.
- [ ] **Benefits concrete:** At least one specific benefit from the approved copy bank is doing real work.
- [ ] **Confidence pointed at:** The copy points, explicitly or implicitly, at a child who feels capable.
- [ ] **Trust shown:** A concrete trust signal appears (account manager, lesson report, 1.3%, vetted tutors, money back guarantee).
- [ ] **Tutor/teacher accuracy:** No blanket "teachers" claim. "Tutors" is the default unless the specific person is a teacher.
- [ ] **Forbidden words check:** No AI, no platform, no app-as-product, no cutting-edge, no sign up, no enrol.
- [ ] **Read aloud test:** Does it sound clear? Would a parent understand it immediately?

---

## 11. What to do when the brief breaks the rules

A user will sometimes ask for something this skill forbids. Push back, don't comply silently.

> "I can't write that as briefed — Tutero parent copy has a hard rule against [the forbidden thing], because [the strategic reason]. Here's what I'd write instead: [the alternative]. If you want me to override, tell me explicitly."

Never silently insert a forbidden word. Always offer an alternative.
